Introduction

Typically, infectious diseases are mathematically characterized by using compartmental models. These models divide the population in compartments, with varying assumptions on each individual as being in various compartments having the same characteristics. These models are often investigated through the use of ordinary differential equations, but can also be viewed in a stochastic framework, which are more akin to the real world but are often harder to analyze.

These models can help us predict properties of the disease as it spreads, giving us vital information regarding the prevalence and duration of the epidemic. Furthermore, by varying hyper-parameters of the model we can simulate various outcomes of the state of the epidemic to determine the most effective course of actions. For instance, some models may take into account equipments such as ICU and vaccines to identify places of aid in the healthcare system.

This project is by no means an exhaustive literature review of the different types of models but it does shed light on variuos techniques that are currently being used. In the project I will use these models as a way of tracking the COVID-19 pandemic.
